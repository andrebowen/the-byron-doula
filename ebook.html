Pregnancy

What do I need to do?

You

Trimester 1 · Weeks 1–13

  • Book your first midwife or OB appointment
  • Start prenatal vitamins (folate, iodine, iron)
  • Confirm your due date
  • Discuss birth setting options
  • Reduce alcohol & caffeine
  • Check Medicare / private health cover
  • Share news with your support people

Trimester 2 · Weeks 14–27

  • Anatomy scan at 18–20 weeks
  • Notify your employer, plan leave
  • Begin a birth preferences document
  • Research and book doula support
  • Start pelvic floor exercises
  • Consider antenatal yoga or hypnobirthing
  • Arrange dental check-up
Your Partner

Trimester 1 · Weeks 1–13

  • Attend the first OB / midwife appointment
  • Read about early pregnancy stages
  • Research paternity / partner leave options
  • Take on extra household tasks
  • Reduce alcohol in solidarity
  • Talk openly about feelings and fears

Trimester 2 · Weeks 14–27

  • Attend the anatomy scan
  • Research prams, car seats, nursery
  • Attend antenatal classes together
  • Help draft the birth plan
  • Confirm your leave dates with employer

Pregnancy

Getting ready for birth

You

Trimester 3 · Weeks 28–40

  • Tour your birth facility
  • Write your birth plan
  • Pack your hospital bag by 36 weeks
  • Install the baby capsule & get it checked
  • Set up the nursery & safe sleep space
  • Attend a birth or hypnobirthing class
  • Discuss pain relief options
  • Freeze meals or organise a meal train
  • Arrange care for older children & pets
  • Confirm your doula is on call from 37 weeks
Your Partner

Trimester 3 · Weeks 28–40

  • Know the route to the hospital
  • Read the birth plan — know it well
  • Pack your own bag (snacks, charger, change of clothes)
  • Install and check the baby capsule
  • Learn comfort & support techniques for labour
  • Save the hospital's number in your phone
  • Know the "when to go in" signs
  • Confirm your leave start date
  • Be on call from 37 weeks

Tip callout — Nathalie's tip

"A birth plan is less about a fixed outcome and more about knowing your options. The more informed you are, the more empowered you'll feel — whatever unfolds."

Birth

What do I need to prepare?

You

Your hospital bag

  • Birth plan — print 2–3 copies
  • Medicare card, ID, health fund details
  • Snacks & electrolyte drink for labour
  • Your own pillow from home
  • Loose, comfortable clothes to labour in
  • Maternity pads & comfortable underwear
  • Phone charger & a comforting playlist
  • Baby's first outfit, nappies & swaddle wraps
  • Going-home outfit for you

Logistics

  • Save the hospital's direct number
  • Know your "when to come in" guidelines
  • Confirm your doula's on-call details
Your Partner

Partner's bag

  • Snacks and water for a long labour
  • Change of clothes
  • Phone charger & portable battery
  • Cash or card for parking
  • Something to do during early labour

Logistics

  • Fuel the car and know the route
  • Arrange backup for pets & older children
  • Have leave approved and ready to start
  • Know who to notify when baby arrives
  • Keep your phone on at all times

Birth

During labour

You

Early labour · At home

  • Rest and conserve your energy
  • Stay hydrated and eat a light meal
  • Time contractions using an app
  • Use heat, a shower, or bath
  • Call your doula when contractions are regular

Active labour · At your birth place

  • Hand your birth plan to the midwife
  • Ask questions before agreeing to interventions
  • Change positions regularly
  • Use breathing, sound, and movement
  • Lean on your doula and support person
  • You can always ask for more time to decide
Your Partner

Early labour · At home

  • Time contractions together
  • Offer heat, water, and food
  • Stay calm and present
  • Contact the doula when needed
  • Load the car and be ready to go

Active labour · At your birth place

  • Be the voice for the birth plan
  • Offer physical comfort — pressure, movement
  • Keep the environment calm and positive
  • Eat and stay hydrated yourself
  • Trust the team — and your partner

Postpartum

What do I need to do?

You

For you · First days home

  • Rest — sleep when baby sleeps
  • Eat warm, nourishing food and stay hydrated
  • Accept help — say yes to everything offered
  • Book your 6-week postnatal check-up
  • Get feeding support early if you need it

For baby · First days home

  • Register the birth (within 60 days in NSW)
  • Book the 2-week midwife visit
  • Newborn bloodspot screening test
  • Hearing screen (done in hospital)
  • Safe sleep setup
Your Partner

First days home

  • Take as much leave as possible
  • Handle all household tasks
  • Manage visitors — protect rest time
  • Learn to settle baby so mum can sleep
  • Do the night feeds where you can
  • Watch for signs of PND in both of you
  • Cook, shop, and keep the home running

Warning callout — seek help immediately if you notice

Heavy bleeding · Fever or chills · Chest pain · Thoughts of harming yourself or your baby · Feeling unable to cope

Postpartum

Weeks 1–6 & beyond

You

Emotional wellbeing

  • Check in with yourself daily
  • Talk to your GP about baby blues or PND
  • Limit visitors if you're overwhelmed
  • Connect with other new mothers
  • Be honest with your partner about how you feel

Physical recovery

  • See a pelvic floor physiotherapist
  • Begin gentle movement from week 3
  • Scar care if you had a caesarean
  • Recheck iron & vitamins at 6 weeks
  • Discuss contraception with your GP
Your Partner

Weeks 1–6

  • Check in daily — emotionally, not just practically
  • Encourage rest, not "bouncing back"
  • Attend the 6-week check-up together
  • Organise extra help if family isn't nearby
  • Make space for connection as a couple
  • Learn the signs of PND — in yourself too
  • Ask how she's really feeling, and listen

Tip callout — Nathalie's tip

"The fourth trimester is real. Healing takes time, and there is no timeline for 'bouncing back.' Prioritise yourself so you can show up for your baby."
